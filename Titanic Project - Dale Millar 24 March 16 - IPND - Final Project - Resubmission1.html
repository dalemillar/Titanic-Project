Titanic Data Analysis¶

Question: What factors made people more likely to survive?¶

Introduction¶

This data set provides insight into 891 passengers of a total of 2200 passengers and crew who were on board the Titanic when it hit an ice-berg on 15 April 1912.

The intent of this analysis is to look at the most important factors that affected the chances of survival of the passengers using a number of pandas and numpy functionalities.

The primary factors that are investigated in this analysis are:

  • Passenger Travel Class - which is seen as a proxy for social class
  • Passenger Gender
  • Passenger Age
  • Passenger Adult/Child - I have taken a Child to be 16 years or below

Key Questions to be Addressed in this Analysis¶

It is highly unlikely that the survival chances of this sample of 891 passengers from a total of 2,200 is utterly random. Even anecdotal evidence available in the referenced documents indicates that there are factors which strongly correlate to survival chances.

In order to provide an objective framework to the analysis, the following questions are posed:

Primary Question¶

What is the factor in the passenger data that influences a person's chances of survival the most heavily - which factor correlates most strongly with chances of survival?

Secondary Question¶

Is there a secondary factor that has strong influence and could be regarded as the next level of some kind of decison making tree?

I will refer back to these questions during the analysis.

Data Wrangling - I¶

The data is not particularly messy. Three areas that are potentially problematic are:

  • There are a number of empty columns, namely [3,4,7,9,10,15] which need to gotten rid of
  • There are white spaces in the Age column which need to be filled with NaNs
  • There is no Child column which is added and populated with Boolean

The titanic_raw dataframe is used as the go to structure throughout the analysis and covers all 891 of the sample.

It should be noted that the titanic_raw_kids dataframe is only 714 passengers long and this is because the white space Age entries are eliminated - of course we cannot tell if these missing 177 passengers may cause distortion in some of the assumptions made when looking at Child survival, but this is noted and then ignored in the analysis.

Statistical Test - Difference in Proportions¶

Firstly look at proportions of survivors in each class:

First Class Survival Rate = 62.96% Second Class Survival Rate = 47.28% Third Class Survival Rate = 24.23%

Now make null hypothesis that First Class Survival Rate >= Second Class Survival Rate and alternative hypothesis that First Class Survival Rate < Second Class Survival Rate

For this analysis, the significance level is 0.05. The test method is a two-proportion z-test.

P=0.5575 SE=0.0498 Z=3.11

From Normal Distribution Calculator to find P(z < -3.11) = 0.00003, and P(z > 3.11) = 0.00003. Thus, the P-value = 0.00006.

Since this P-Value is lower than the significance value of 0.05, we can fail to reject the null hypothesis.

Problems with the Data¶

The following issues with the data are important:

  • There are 177 age records missing from the data set - my solution to his was to elilminate them from my age survival analysis. I kept the records in the dataframe for all the rest of the analysis which seems to be sensible - there is little point in throwing data away when you don't need to. For the age analysis, it would have been possible to add in random age data - I chose not to do this becasue it seemed utterly superfluous. The only problem is that I think the missing age data will be highly skued towards 3rd Class passengers probably giving fairly distorted results for our age analysis. Just to check this:

  • I have made a lot of assumptions about First Class passengers being close to the upper decks and Third Class being locked away in Steerage. This is anecdotal and cannot be verified by the data set. We could learn a lot from finding out where people were located in terms of accommodation on the ship. Maybe deck numbers would help here.
  • I cannot find any mention of the crew in the data set and I think it would have been quite interesting to see what their survival rates were like.
  • I have used Descriptive Statistics to do this analysis with out any Statistical Testing to speak of. This means that one cannot use these findings to infer what could be the survival chances of the other 1,400 or so passengers.

Conclusions¶

From the analysis, the overwhelming factor in surviving the Titanic was being female. If you were lucky enough to be a lady travelling in First Class, your chances were greatly enahnced; there is a very clear link between class of travel and survival rate.

Gender Influence¶

Females make up 35.2% and males 64.8% of the overall passenger sample. The extremely interesting statisitc is that the female survival rate is an astonishing 68.1% of total survivors - almost double the female proportion of the total sample. There is therefore very compelling evidence that women were given priority in the lifeboats (there is no proof of this in the data sets).

Class Influence¶

Having completed the grouping, we can see the 24.2%, 20.7% and 55.1% breakdown of 1st, 2nd and 3rd classes across the entire sample.

Third Class passengers, despite making up 55% of total passengers, only make up 34% of survivors - the most obvious rationale is that these passengers were in 'steerage' well below deck and struggled to get out of the ship - this is an assertion which is not substantiated by location information.

First and Second Class passengers (Respective Survival Rates of 34.8% and 25.4%) greatly outstrip their overall proportion of the sample (24.2% and 20.7% respectively). Again, the unassertable assumption is that these passengers had more access to the upper decks and therefore the lifeboats.

Somewhat surprisingly, the age of passengers and whether or not they are children has a smaller impact on their ability to survive.

Age Influence¶

Although overall age influence across the sample was surprisingly indeterminite, the 14% proportion of children in the overall sample was greatly outstripped by the 19% survival rate of children in total survivors. This indicates that children were given priority in lifeboats but there is no real prrof of this in the data set.

Primary Question¶

What is the factor in the passenger data that influences a person's chances of survival the most heavily - which factor correlates most strongly with chances of survival?

The most important factor in survival is gender.

Secondary Question¶

Is there a secondary factor that has strong influence and could be regarded as the next level of some kind of decison making tree?

The secondary most important factor is class of travel.
